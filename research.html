Energy Transmission in Extreme Environments

Electric energy transmission in deep-sea, space, and highly strained platforms requires interconnects that can survive pressure, temperature cycling, low-pressure conditions, and repeated deformation. In these environments, small interfacial failures can propagate quickly and lead to unstable device performance.

Our work focuses on materials selection, interface engineering, and process design that maintain conductivity while minimizing mechanical and thermal mismatch. Students can participate from early-stage failure analysis to fabrication and reliability validation, producing data that guides robust device architecture for long-duration operation.

  • Define failure modes in harsh mechanical and thermal environments.
  • Develop soft/robust interconnect fabrication strategies.
  • Benchmark reliability for long-duration operation.

Ion-Based Energy Control in Polymer Systems

Many energy devices rely on ionic species for charge storage, but ionic transport is slower than electronic transport, which limits direct use in fast control electronics. At the same time, local ion concentration gradients and ion-polymer interactions can generate useful memory and thermoelectric behavior that electronic-only systems cannot easily reproduce.

We investigate ion-gel composition, polymer-network structure, and 3D processing conditions to control ionic dynamics in a predictable way. The goal is to translate these material-level effects into stable, tunable device functions for sensing, adaptive control, and next-generation low-power platforms.

  • Engineer ion transport pathways in polymer matrices.
  • Link composition and process to switching/thermal response.
  • Translate ionic effects into controllable device functions.

Wide-Bandgap Routes for High-Power Electronics

Growing demand from electric vehicles, AI servers, and large-scale power systems is pushing devices beyond the operating range of conventional silicon. Wide-bandgap systems such as GaN and SiC offer strong potential for high-voltage and high-temperature operation, but scalable fabrication routes remain a central challenge.

Our lab explores liquid-metal-enabled processing in ambient environments to reduce manufacturing barriers while preserving performance. We study composite design, interface control, and process integration so that high-power materials can move more efficiently from laboratory demonstrations to deployable device modules.

  • Address high-voltage/high-temperature material constraints.
  • Use liquid-metal-assisted fabrication in ambient conditions.
  • Target scalable processing for power-device integration.
